Amazon, But Make It User-Friendly

August 2020

There are many things which just make sense in this world, even during the chaotic era we’re currently living in. To give you some simple examples: If you knock over a full cup of water, it will spill. If you close your eyes, you won’t see anything. If you stub your toe, it will hurt.

The Amazon website is not one of these things (at least from a UX perspective).

This gem was found on the product page for a mouse pad. I'm not lying.

There is so much “gunk” on Amazon’s website, which I assume was added to give users more pathways for finding items, but probably primarily to maximize the number of products they see on a single page... leading them to buy more stuff.

My goal here is not to make users buy more stuff, but to make the experience a little bit cleaner and easier for them, which is why Amazon and I could probably never be friends IRL. Sorry, Jeff.

New and Improved

Let’s take a look at the home page. Immediately, we can see Amazon is trying to throw random crap at us. A Wireless sound bar? I’m sure it’ll pair amazingly with my 10 year old TV with no wireless capability. Add to cart.

If you want me to sign up for Prime Video, pick something other than Rambo: Last Blood for the ads.

We can also see the categories are hidden away in a hamburger menu, which I’ll admit, I never cared about until now since I always use the search bar. Regardless, Amazon should be focused on helping the user find what they need, not selling them on things they don’t.

I redesigned this page mostly by cleaning up the UI and reorganizing the categories (I really did go through every single category, folks).

A look into the 14 revamped categories of stuff [you don't really need].
